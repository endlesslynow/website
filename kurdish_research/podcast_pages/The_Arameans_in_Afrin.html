The Arameans in Afrin

Download Audio File

Description

The provided text explores the historical presence and evolving identity of Arameans in Syria's Afrin District, particularly up to 2010, highlighting a paradox between their deep historical legacy and their minimal modern demographic presence. It traces the Arameans' ancient origins as a prominent Semitic people whose Aramaic language became the Near East's lingua franca, even as they lacked a unified empire. The text then details their transformation into "Syriac" Christians, explaining how this exonym became an accepted self-identifier and acknowledging the complex modern identity debate among Aramean, Syriac, and Assyrian groups. Furthermore, it showcases the region's rich archaeological sites, like the Ain Dara temple and various early Christian "Dead Cities," as irrefutable evidence of Afrin's significance to Aramean and Syriac Christian civilization. Despite this profound history, the district was overwhelmingly Kurdish by 2010, with a small Christian population comprising both historic denominations and recent Kurdish converts, coexisting peacefully under the Ba'athist regime's anti-minority policies.
