Description

The provided text offers a detailed analysis of the reign of Sultan Selim III (1789–1807), portraying him as an Enlightened reformer whose ambitious plans were tragically undone by the forces of chaos. The source primarily focuses on his comprehensive reform program, the Nizam-ı Cedid (New Order), which sought to modernize the Ottoman state by creating a new, European-style army and establishing permanent embassies, financed by a separate treasury. However, the reforms faced fatal opposition from an internal "triangle of opposition" consisting of the entrenched Janissary corps, the conservative religious scholars (ulama), and powerful provincial warlords (ayan). This internal strife, compounded by the volatile Napoleonic Wars and a disastrous series of foreign policy reversals, culminated in the 1807 coup and the Sultan's brutal assassination, cementing his legacy as a martyr whose failure ultimately paved the way for his successor's more ruthless, successful reforms.
