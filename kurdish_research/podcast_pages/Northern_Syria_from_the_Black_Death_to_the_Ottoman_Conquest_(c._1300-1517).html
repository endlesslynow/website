Northern Syria from the Black Death to the Ottoman Conquest (c. 1300-1517)

Download Audio File

Description

The provided academic excerpts offer a detailed historical analysis of the Mamluk Sultanate's Northern Syrian frontier between the fourteenth and sixteenth centuries, focusing on the region's irreversible decline. The central argument posits that the initial strength and prosperity of the Mamluk state were fundamentally broken by two major shocks: the Black Death starting in 1347, which caused catastrophic rural depopulation and collapse of the agrarian economy, and the Timurid invasion of 1400–1401, which destroyed urban centers and depleted skilled labor. The sources emphasize that the subsequent fifteenth century was defined by a protracted crisis, characterized by the dissolution of central Mamluk authority and financial insolvency due to the failure of the iqta' land-grant system. This sustained institutional weakness and military stagnation ultimately rendered the frontier indefensible against the technologically superior Ottoman Empire, leading to its swift conquest in 1516.
