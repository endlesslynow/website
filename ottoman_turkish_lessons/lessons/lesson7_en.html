Lesson 7
The verb 'To Have'

Lesson Objectives

  • Understand how the verb 'To HAVE' is expressed in Turkish based on the definiteness of the object.
  • Learn to use وار var (present/existent) and یوق yoq (absent/non-existent) for indefinite objects.
  • Learn the two main constructions for 'To HAVE' with indefinite objects:
    • Subject in Genitive + Object with Possessive Affix + وار/یوق.
    • Subject in Locative + Object + وار/یوق.
  • Learn to use the substantive verb در dir for definite objects.
  • Understand word order differences for definite and indefinite objects.
  • Conjugate 'To HAVE' in Present and Past (Affirmative, Negative, Interrogative) for both indefinite and definite objects.
  • Learn expressions for 'any', 'not any', 'how many', 'how much', 'some', 'both', 'either/or', 'neither/nor'.
  • Familiarize with new vocabulary.

§ 118. The English verb 'To HAVE' is expressed in Turkish in two ways, according to the object of the verb.

If the object is indefinite the adjectives وار var 'present: existent' and یوق yoq 'absent: non-existent' are used to express that sense. These may be followed by the verbal particle of affirmative در dir; which in this case, as in many other cases may be omitted in conversation (§ 76).

If the object is definite the Substantive verb is employed (§ 127).

1. The verb To HAVE with an Indefinite Object.

§ 119. In such phrases as: I have a book, he has a dog, it is expressed in two ways.

I. By putting the subject in the Genitive, followed by the object with the possessive affix and the verb وار var, وار در var dir; یوق yoq, یوقدر yoqdour; as:

بنم بر کتابم واردر bénim bir kitabım vardır I have a book.
بابامك بر کتابی یوقدر babamın bir kitabı yoqdour My father does not have a book.

Literally: of me there is a book, of my father there is no book.

§ 120. Sometimes the subject, when a pronoun, is omitted, especially when the subject is not accented or emphasized: then the affix of the object indicates the subject (§§ 70, 102); as:

بر کتابم وار در bir kitabım var dir I have a book.

The affix shows the person of the subject.

§ 121. When the subject is a noun it is always considered as in the third person, therefore the object must end with the pronominal affix of the third person, ی (i) or سی (si).

افندينك بر اچی واردر Efféndiniñ bir évi vardır The gentleman has a house.
چوجوغك بر الماسی وار Chojoughouñ bir élması var The boy has an apple.

The words افندی efféndi, چوجوق chojouq being substantives, are of course in the third person.

II. The verb To HAVE with an indefinite object is rendered in Turkish in another way also. In the first way the subject was in the Genitive case; in the second, the subject must be put in the Locative; as:

§ 122.

بنده بر کتاب وار در béndé bir kitab var dir I have a book.
پدرمدہ بر قلم وار در pédérimdé bir qalém var dir My father has a pen.

§ 123. Although it is not very correct grammatically, there is a custom among the common people not to append to the noun the possessive affixes of the first and second persons plural. Instead of saying correctly سزك آتكز Siziñ atıñız, بزم اومز bizim évimiz, they say سزك آت Siziñ at, بزم او bizim év just as in English. بزم اوك پنجره‌سی Bizim éviñ pénjéréssi the window of our house, for بزم اومزك پنجره‌سی Bizim évimiziñ pénjéréssi. بزم پدر Bizim pédér our father, for بزم پدرمز Bizim pédérimiz, or merely پدر pédér; as: بزم پدرمز اوده در bizim pédérimiz évde dir, پدر اوده در pédér évde dir or بزم پدر اوده در bizim pédér évde dir (Our father is in the house).

§ 124. The Plural Locative forms of the Personal Pronouns sometimes give the sense of ‘house, home’. بزده بر اينك وار Bizdé bir i-nék var means both ‘We have a cow’ and ‘There is a cow in our house’. Lit.: ‘in us’.

§ 125. But the rendering for nouns is different: ‘at my father’s’ or ‘in my father’s house’, ‘the people of my father’s house’, are expressed by adding گیل gil; as:

بابام گیلbabam gilThe people of my father’s house, my father’s family.
باجناغم گیلدهbajanaghim gildéAt my brother-in-law’s house.
همشیره‌م گیلنهhémshirém giléTo my sister’s.
دايم گیل بزده درdayim gil bizdé dirThe family of my uncle is in our house.

§ 126. “There is, there are’ is rendered by the Locative with وار var, یوق yoq dour (§ 76). But اوندا وار onda var, بنده‌ده وار béndé var, denote possession; as: اوده بر آت وار Évdé bir at var There is a horse in the house. But بنده‌ بر آت وار Béndé bir at var I have a horse. In the first sentence it expresses location and in the second possession.

2. The verb To HAVE with a Definite Object.

§ 127. When the object of the verb To HAVE is definite, it is rendered in Turkish by the substantive verb در dir (§ 118).

§ 128. The order of the construction is this: first comes the object, then the subject, and the verb in the third place.

§ 129. This is a general rule in the Ottoman-Turkish language. In every case when the object is indefinite, the subject comes first; and when the object is definite the object comes first; Ex.:

بنم بر کتابم وار درbénim bir kitabım var dirI have a book.
کتاب بنده درkitab béndé dirI have the book.

In the first instance the object (a book) is indefinite, therefore the subject comes first; in the second the object (the book) is definite, therefore the object comes first and the subject follows it.

Remarks and Special Expressions

§ 130. Remarks: The English Conjunction ‘but’ is expressed either by putting the Arabic words لكن lakin, اما ém'-ma or am'ma, فقط faqat or the Turkish ایسه ده i-sé dé, all meaning ‘but’ (§ 239, 476); as:

پارم وار اما آز درparam var am'ma az dirI have money, but it is little.
پارم وار لكن آز درparam var lakin az dir
پارم وار فقط آز درparam var faqat az dir
پارم وار ایسه ده آز درparam var i-sé dé az dir

§ 131. “Any” is expressed in Turkish in two ways: one by هیچ hich, and the other without using that word, but by simply using the object of the verb (§ 188); as:

سنده هیچ آكمك وار میدر؟séndé hich ékmék var mıdır?Have you any bread?
اونك هیچ پاره‌سی یوقدرonuñ hich parası yoqdour.He does not have any money.

§ 132. “Not any, not at all” is expressed by هیچ hich.

هیچ پاره‌سی یوقدرhich parası yoqdour;He has not any money;
هیچ ایو دکل چوق خسته درhich éyi déyil choq hasta dir.He is not at all well: he is very sick.

§ 133. (§ 174). Ex.: “How many?” is expressed by قاچ qach?

قاچ غروشڭ وار در؟qach ghroushuñ var dar?How many piastres do you have?
قاچ کتابی وار در؟qach kitabı var dar?How many books does he have?

§ 134. “How much?” is expressed by نه قدر né qadar? (§ 179); as:

نه قدر شكرڭ وار؟né qadar shékériñ var?How much sugar do you have?
نه قدر آكمكمز وار؟né qadar ékmégimiz var?How much bread do we have?

§ 135. “Some” is expressed by بر آز bir az ‘a little, a small piece of anything’, in reference to inanimate objects (§ 182); as: بر آز آكمك bir az ékmék some bread.

But in reference to animate objects بعض ba‘zı, بر قاچ bir qach is used (§ 181); as:

بر قاچ آدمارbir qach ademlérsome people.
بعض افنديلرba‘zı éfféndilérsome gentlemen.
بر قاچ حیوانلرbir qach haïvanlarsome animals.

§ 136. “Both” is rendered by هم ... هم hém — hém (§ 469); as:

بنده هم آكمك و هم طوز وارbéndé hém ékmék vé hém touz var.I have both bread and salt.
خالمك هم کاغذی وهم قلمی وار درKhalamıñ hém kaghadı vé hém qalemi var dir.My aunt has both paper and pen.

§ 137. “Either ... or ...” is rendered by یا ... یا ya—ya-; “Neither ... nor ...” is rendered by نه ... نه né—né (§ 472); as:

بنده نه آكمك وار نه طوزbéndé né ékmék var né touz.I have neither bread nor salt.
سنده یا قلم وار یا کاغدséndé ya qalém var ya kaghad.You (sg.) have either pen or paper.
