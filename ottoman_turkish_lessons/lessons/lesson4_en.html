1. The Six Cases

There are two number styles in Ottoman: Singular and Plural; And six cases, namely: the Nominative, Genitive, Dative, Accusative, Locative and Ablative cases.

The Nominative Case

The Nominative case (or the Subject) answers to the questions: who? or what? كیم (kim?) نه (né?) as the subject of the verb; as: Who is learning? — The boy اوغلان (oghlan).

The Genitive Case

The Genitive (or Possessive) case answers to the questions: whose? or of which? كیمك (kimin?) نه نك (nénin). Ex.: Whose book? — The boy's book اوغلانك كتابى (oghlanın kitabı).

The Dative Case

The Dative answers to the questions: to whom? to which? كیمه (kimé?) نه یه (né-yé?) Ex.: To whom shall I give it? — To the boy اوغلانه (oghlana).

The Accusative Case

The Accusative (or Objective case) marks the object of an action, and answers to the questions: whom? or what? كیمی (kimi?) نه یی (né-yi?) Ex.: Whom or what do you see? — I see the boy, the house اوغلانی (oghlanı), اوی (évi).

The Locative Case

The Locative answers to the questions: where? wherein? نرده (nérdé?) Ex.: Where is the boy? — He is in the school مكتبده (méktébdé).

The Ablative Case

The Ablative answers to the questions: from whom? from what? كیمدن (kimdén?) ندن (nédén?) Ex.: From whom did you take this book? — From the boy اوغلاندن (oghlandan).

The Four Forms of Declension

Basicaly, for most nouns ending in consonants (First Form), the basic pattern is:

  1. Nominative: Base form (no suffix)
  2. Genitive: Add: -in/-ın/-un/-ün
  3. Dative: Add: -e/-a
  4. Accusative: Add: -i/-ı/-u/-ü
  5. Locative: Add: -de/-da
  6. Ablative: Add: -den/-dan

For nouns ending in vowels (Fourth Form), the basic pattern is different as they need buffer consonants to avoid awkward pronunciation when endings are added:

  1. Nominative: Base form (no suffix)
  2. Genitive: Add: -nın/-nin/-nun/-nün (adding 'n' as a buffer)
  3. Dative: Add: -ya/-ye (adding 'y' as a buffer)
  4. Accusative: Add: -yı/-yi/-yu/-yü (adding 'y' as a buffer)
  5. Locative: Add: -da/-de (no buffer needed)
  6. Ablative: Add: -dan/-den (no buffer needed)

Note; The declension patterns shown in the lesson are for nouns, not verbs, and they remain constant regardless of the tense of the sentence. For example:

Present Tense: I see the boy بن اوغلانی گوریورم
Ben oghlanı görüyorum
Past Tense: I saw the boy بن اوغلانی گوردم
Ben oghlanı gördüm
Future Tense: I will see the boy بن اوغلانی گوره‌جغم
Ben oghlanı göreceğim

Second Form

The second form of declension comprises all consonants ending in ق q. The difference from the first declension is this, that ق q is changed into غ gh, when it is followed by a vowel. Ex.:

باليق ba-lıq: here ق q is not followed by a vowel, because it stands at the end of the syllable. باليغا ba-lı-gha: here the third syllable begins with ق q and is vowelled, therefore it changes into غ gh, thus we have باليغه ba-lı-gha. This change takes place in the Genitive, Dative and Accusative cases; in the Locative and Ablative cases and in the plural the ق q remains unchanged, because in those cases q is not followed by a vowel.

Note. In Arabic and Persian words and in all words borrowed from foreign languages, the ق q remains unchanged.

Third Form

The third form contains all the soft syllabled nouns ending in ك k. The letter k is changed into y, because it is vowelled: that is, when the syllable would otherwise begin with ك k, the k is changed into y. Ex.: اوردك ör-dék: here ك k is not vowelled, it is at the end of the second syllable. اوردكه ör-dé-ké is wrong, for the last syllable would begin with ك k; therefore the k must be changed into y, اوردكه ör-dé-yé. This is noticed only in the pronunciation forms for k and y.

In the plural and in the Locative and Ablative cases k is unchangeable, as a vowel does not immediately follow the k.

Note. اوق oq arrow, طوق toq satiated, قرق qırq forty, يوك yük load, كوك kök a root, are exceptions to the above rules, as they do not change q into gh, nor k into y.

Fourth Form

The fourth form comprises all nouns ending in the vowel letters ا و ى. In the singular, the Genitive is formed by adding نك -nın; in the Dative يه -yé is added to the Nom., in the Acc. يی -yi. No change takes place in the remaining cases or in the plural.

When a word ending in a vowel receives a grammatical ending beginning with a vowel, a hiatus results, which is practically a difficulty in pronunciation. This is very common in Ottoman. To avoid this difficulty it is usual to insert a consonant ى y (and only in the Genitive n. This is really the retention of part of the original genitive termination -nın).
