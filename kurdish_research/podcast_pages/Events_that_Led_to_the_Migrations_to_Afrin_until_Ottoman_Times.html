Description

This academic report explores the history of migration and demographic change in the Afrin region of northwestern Syria, examining its evolution from ancient times through the pre-Ottoman era. It highlights the region's strategic importance due to its geography and location on trade routes, detailing the settlement of various groups including indigenous communities, Hellenistic settlers, Romans, Arabs, Kurds, Christians, and Yazidis. The study notes how imperial policies, like military colonization, and significant events, such as the Crusades and Mongol invasions, profoundly shaped the local population mix. Ultimately, it portrays Afrin on the eve of Ottoman rule as a complex mosaic of diverse ethnic and religious communities with established identities and local power structures, particularly among Kurdish and Yazidi groups in the mountainous areas.
