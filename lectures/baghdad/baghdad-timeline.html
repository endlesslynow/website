BAGHDAD

Center of the World

Revolution and Mudbrick

650-768

Before Baghdad became the Abbasid world's glittering capital, it was the answer to a political problem. The Abbasids rose through secret networks, black banners, Khorasanian armies, and the collapse of Umayyad Damascus. Al-Mansur then turned that revolutionary victory into a city of fire-marked geometry, mudbrick walls, river trade, and imperial control.

The Golden Age

8th - 13th Century

Baghdad grew beyond its circular walls into one of the great intellectual centers of world history. Its golden age was not a single calm century of scholars at desks, but a long Abbasid story of imperial wealth, palace politics, civil war, translation, science, floods, foreign military rule, and stubborn institutional survival. By the time the Mongols arrived in 1258, the city was politically weakened, but its schools, hospitals, libraries, and memories of universal learning still mattered across the Islamic world.

The Mongol Siege

1219 - 1358

The fall of Baghdad in 1258 was more than a single battle. It was the final collision between a weakened Abbasid capital and the expanding Mongol world empire. The city still carried the memory of universal learning and caliphal authority, but Hulagu's campaign exposed how fragile that authority had become. The siege destroyed the Abbasid caliphate in Baghdad and reduced the former capital of the Islamic world to a wounded provincial city that slowly rebuilt without regaining its old supremacy.

Stagnation & Invasions

1258 - 1534

After the Mongol conquest, Baghdad survived without recovering its old imperial role. The city rebuilt fragments of trade and religious life, then suffered Timur's devastating attacks and a long age of rule by Jalayirid, Black Sheep, White Sheep, and Safavid powers. This was not an empty pause in history; it was the period when Baghdad became a symbolic prize between regional and imperial forces.

Ottoman Rule

1528 - 1917

Ottoman Baghdad was not simply a long sleepy decline. It began with a fragile local coup, became a fortress in the Ottoman-Safavid frontier, developed a semi-independent Mamluk political order, and then entered the nineteenth century as a city pulled into steam navigation, telegraphy, education reform, railway rivalry, oil politics, and world war.

Modern Era

1950s Prelude – Present

Modern Baghdad moved from late-monarchical optimism to revolution, Baathist dictatorship, war, sanctions, occupation, sectarian violence, protest, and uneven recovery. Its modern history is not only a sequence of rulers. It is also the story of new districts, monuments, disappearing communities, damaged heritage, literary survival, and citizens repeatedly returning to the city's public spaces.

Protest, Recovery, and a Contested Future

2003 – Present

After the 2003 invasion, Baghdad entered one of the most turbulent periods in its modern history — occupation, sectarian war, the rise and defeat of ISIS, and the devastating 2019 Tishreen protests. Yet the city has continuously reasserted itself: its youth returned to its squares, its artists painted its walls, and its residents rebuilt life in neighborhoods others had written off. Baghdad's story did not end; it became contested, plural, and stubbornly alive.
