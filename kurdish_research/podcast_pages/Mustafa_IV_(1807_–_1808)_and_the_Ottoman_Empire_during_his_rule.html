Mustafa IV (1807 – 1808) and the Ottoman Empire during his rule

Download Audio File

Description

The provided text presents a detailed historical analysis of the tumultuous 1807–1808 crisis in the Ottoman Empire, focusing on the brief and chaotic reign of Sultan Mustafa IV. The sources outline how the disastrous English Event, where a British fleet breached the Dardanelles, led to the collapse of public confidence in Sultan Selim III and facilitated the Kabakçı Mustafa Rebellion, which enthroned the anti-reformist Mustafa IV. Mustafa’s authority was quickly reduced to a "symbolic one" after he was forced to grant the rebels legal immunity, while internationally, his regime was betrayed by the Treaty of Tilsit, leaving the empire isolated. This weakness allowed the powerful provincial notable, Alemdar Mustafa Pasha, to march on the capital to restore Selim, prompting Mustafa IV's fratricidal order to execute the two remaining princes before his own deposition and subsequent execution, securing the throne for Mahmud II. The entire crisis served as a bloody lesson that ultimately made the destruction of the entrenched Janissary corps inevitable.
