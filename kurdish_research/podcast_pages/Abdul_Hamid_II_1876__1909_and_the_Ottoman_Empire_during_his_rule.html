Abdul Hamid II 1876 1909 and the Ottoman Empire during his rule

Description

The provided text offers an extensive historical analysis of the polarizing reign of Sultan Abdul Hamid II (1876–1909), defining it as a pivotal epoch characterized by autocratic modernization and internal crisis within the late Ottoman Empire. The document details the Sultan’s paranoia-driven method of rule from his isolated Yıldız Palace, which relied on a massive Hafiye spy network and stringent censorship to centralize authority and suppress political opposition. Economically, the era was defined by financial insolvency and foreign control, exemplified by the crippling function of the Ottoman Public Debt Administration (OPDA). Yet, despite being labeled the "Red Sultan" for the Hamidian Massacres of Armenians, he simultaneously drove a frenetic program of modernization, expanding technical education and promoting Pan-Islamism through projects like the Hejaz Railway as a geopolitical strategy. Ultimately, his attempts to strengthen the empire through absolutism led to the rise of the educated military class that would execute the Young Turk Revolution and depose him in 1909.
