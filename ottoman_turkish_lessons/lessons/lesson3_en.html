Past Tense of the Substantive Verb

The Past Tense of the substantive verb (to be) is formed using the suffixes attached to the past stem ايد (id).

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
بن ايدم bén idim I was
سن ايدك sén idin you were (singular/informal)
او ايدى o idi he/she/it was
بز ايدك biz idik we were
سز ايدكز siz idiniz you were (plural/formal)
انلر ايديلر onlar idiler they were

The Negative Past Tense

The negative past tense is formed by using دكل (déyil) before the past tense forms.

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
بن دكل ايدم bén déyil idim I was not
سن دكل ايدك sén déyil idin you were not (singular/informal)
او دكل ايدى o déyil idi he/she/it was not
بز دكل ايدك biz déyil idik we were not
سز دكل ايدكز siz déyil idiniz you were not (plural/formal)
انلر دكل ايديلر onlar déyil idiler they were not

The Interrogative Forms

Interrogative forms are created by inserting the question particle می (mi) between the pronoun and the verb.

Interrogative Past Tense

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
بنمى ايدم؟ Bén mi idim? Was it I?
سنمى ايدك؟ sén mi idin? Was it you? (singular)
اومى ايدى؟ o mou idi? Was it he/she/it?
بزمى ايدك؟ Biz mi idik? Was it we?
سزمى ايدكز؟ siz mi idiniz? Was it you? (plural)
انلرمى ايديلر؟ onlar mi idiler? Was it they?

Interrogative Negative Forms

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
بن دكل مى ايدم؟ Bén déyil mi idim? Was I not?
سن دكل مى ايدك؟ sén déyil mi idin? Were you not? (singular)
او دكل مى ايدى؟ o déyil mi idi? Was he/she/it not?
بز دكل مى ايدك؟ Biz déyil mi idik? Were we not?
سز دكل مى ايدكز؟ siz déyil mi idiniz? Were you not? (plural)
انلر دكل مى ايديلر؟ onlar déyil mi idiler? Were they not?

The Numerals

The Numerals are used just like all other adjectives. Like them, they precede the noun. The noun qualified by cardinals always remains in the singular.

Examples:

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
بر آدم bir adém a man
ايكى چوجوق iki chojouq two boys

Cardinal Numbers 1-12

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
بر bir one
ايكى iki two

Expressing "Half"

The English word "half" is expressed in two ways, by ياريم (yarim) and by بوچوق (bouchouq).

Yarim

Yarim is used before a noun like an adjective:

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
ياريم كون yarim gün half a day
ياريم ساعت yarim sa'at half an hour
ياريم الما yarim alma half an apple

Bouchouq

Bouchouq is always used in connection with a number:

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
ايكى بوچوق iki bouchouq two and a half
اوچ بوچوق ساعت üch bouchouq sa'at three hours and a half
آلتى بوچوق كون alti bouchouq gün six days and a half

"There is, there are"

The English phrase "there is, there are" etc. is expressed in Ottoman by وار (var) 'there is, exists'; its negative being يوق (yoq) 'there is not'.

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
وار، وار در var, var dir there is
يوق، يوقدر yoq, yoq dour there is not
وار ايدى var idi, varidi there was
يوق ايدى yoq idi there was not

Examples with the Verb of Existence:

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
بر كتاب وار (در) bir kitab var (dir) there is a book
بر كتاب وار ايدى bir kitab var idi there was a book
بر كتاب يوق bir kitab yoq there is not a book
بر كتاب يوق ايدى bir kitab yoq idi there was not a book

The Locative Case

The Locative case is made by the addition of ده (dé, da) to the end of the word.

Examples:

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
اوده évdé in the house
كتابده kitabda' in the book
باغچه‌ده bah'-jé-dé in the garden

Examples with the Verb of Existence:

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
اوده بر آدم وار در Evdé bir adém var, dir there is a man in the house
باغچه‌ده چيچك يوقدر Bah'-jé-dé chichék yoq dour there are no flowers in the garden
باغچه‌ده بر كل وار ايدى Bah'-jé-dé bir gül var idi there was a rose in the garden

Asking for the Time

In asking the hour, it is said:

Ottoman Turkish Latin Script English
ساعت قاچدر؟ sa-at qach'dir? What o'clock is it?
ساعت ايكيدر sa-at iki'dir it is two o'clock
