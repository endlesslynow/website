Abdul Hamid I (1774 – 1789) and the Ottoman Empire during his rule

Download Audio File

Description

The source provides a comprehensive analysis of the turbulent reign of Ottoman Sultan Abdul Hamid I (1774–1789), characterizing his rule as a period of accelerating decline defined by a profound mismatch between the sovereign and the systemic crisis he faced. Due to his 44 years of confinement in the Kafes, the Sultan was pious and pacifist, but utterly inexperienced, which hampered his efforts to govern an empire on the brink of collapse. The text highlights how the disastrous Treaty of Küçük Kaynarca (1774) fundamentally compromised Ottoman sovereignty and led directly to the annexation of Crimea by Russia. Furthermore, despite genuine but fragile military and financial reforms (such as the creation of the Esham debt system), the Sultan’s paranoia led him to execute his top reformer, triggering a "war party" that plunged the empire into the ultimately fatal Russo-Turkish War of 1787–1792, the catastrophic news of which caused the Sultan’s death.
