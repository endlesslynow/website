The verb "To Have" - Two Ways

The English verb "To Have" is expressed in Turkish in two ways, according to the object of the verb.

If the object is indefinite the adjectives وار (var) "present; existent" and یوق (yoq) "absent; non-existent" are used to express that sense. These may be followed by the verbal particle of affirmative در (dir), which in this case, as in many other cases may be omitted in conversation.

If the object is definite the Substantive verb is employed.

1. The verb "To Have" with an Indefinite Object

In such phrases as: I have a book, he has a dog, it is expressed in two ways.

Method I: Subject in Genitive

By putting the subject in the Genitive, followed by the object with the possessive affix and the verb وار در, وار (var dir, var); یوق, یوق دور (yoq, yoq dour); as:

Ottoman TurkishTransliterationTranslation
بنم بر کتابم واردر benim bir kitabım vardır I have a book
بابامنك بر کتابی یوقدور babamın bir kitabı yoqdour My father has not a book

Literally: of me there is a book, of my father there is no book.

Sometimes the subject, when a pronoun, is omitted, especially when the subject is not accented or emphasized: then the affix of the object indicates the subject; as:

Ottoman TurkishTransliterationTranslation
بر کتابم وار در bir kitabım var dir I have a book

The affix shows the person of the subject.

When the subject is a noun, the subject is always considered as in the third person, therefore the object must end with the pronominal affix of the third person, س or سی (ı or si).

Ottoman TurkishTransliterationTranslation
افندینك بر اوی واردر Efendinin bir evi vardır The gentleman has a house
چوجوقون بر آلماسی وار در Çocuqoun bir elması var dir The boy has an apple

Method II: Subject in Locative

The verb "To Have" with an indefinite object is rendered in Turkish in another way also. In the first case the subject was in the Genitive case; in the second, the subject must be put in the Locative; as:

Ottoman TurkishTransliterationTranslation
بنده بر کتاب وار در bende bir kitab var dir I have a book
پدرمده بر قلم وار در pederimde bir qalem var dir My father has a pen

Additional Notes on Usage

Although it is not very correct grammatically, it is a custom among the common people not to append to the noun the possessive affixes of the first and second persons in the plural. Instead of saying correctly Sizin atınız, bizim evimiz, they say Sizin at, bizim ev; just as in English.

The Plural Locative forms of the Personal Pronouns sometimes give the sense of "house, home". بزده بر اینك وار (Bizde bir inek var) means both "We have a cow" and "There is a cow in our house".

But the rendering for nouns is different: "at my father's" or "in my father's house", "the people of my father's house", are expressed by adding گل (gil); as:

Ottoman TurkishTransliterationTranslation
بابم گل babam gil at my father's house, my father's family
بایاغمل گیلده bayağıml gilde at my brother-in-law's house
دایمل گیلده dayıml gilde at or our sister's
دایم گل بزده در dayım gil bizde dir The family of my uncle is in our house

"There is, there are" is rendered by the Locative with وار در یوق در (var dir, yoq dour). But onda var, bende var, denote possession; as: اوده بر آت وار (Evde bir at var) "There is a horse in the house". But بنده بر آت وار (Bende bir at var) "I have a horse". In the first sentence it expresses location and in the second possession.

2. The verb "To Have" with a Definite Object

When the object of the verb "To Have" is definite, it is rendered in Turkish by the substantive verb در (dir).

The order of the construction is this: first comes the object, then the subject, and the verb in the third place.

This is a general rule in the Ottoman-Turkish language. In every case where the object is indefinite, the subject comes first; and when the object is definite the object comes first; Ex.:

StructureOttoman TurkishTranslation
Indefinite object بنم بر کتاب benim bir kitab I have a book
Definite object کتاب بنم در kitab benim dir I have the book

In the first instance the object (a book) is indefinite, therefore the subject comes first; in the second the object (the book) is definite, therefore the object comes first and the subject follows it.

Special Usage: "any", "some", "both", etc.

Remarks: The English Conjunction "but" is expressed either by putting the Arabic words امّا (ammā) or لکن (lākin), faqaṭ or the Turkish سه دٔل (i-sé dé), all meaning "but"; as:

Ottoman TurkishTransliterationTranslation
پارم وار امّا آز در param var ammā az dir I have but a little money
پارم وار لکن آز در param var lākin az dir I have but a little money
پارم وار فقط آز در param var faqaṭ az dir I have but a little money
پارم وار سه دٔل آز در param var i-sé dé az dir I have but a little money

"Any"

"Any" is expressed in Turkish in two ways: one by using حیچ (hīch), and the other without using that word, but by simply using the object of the verb; as:

EnglishOttoman TurkishTransliteration
Have you any bread? حیچ آکمکك وار در hīch ékmégın var dır?
Have you any bread? سنده آکمک وار در sende ékmek var dır?
He has not any money آنده حیچ یوق قوندو onda hīch yoq qhoundou
He has not any money آنده یوق در onda pul yoq dur

"Not any, not at all"

"Not any, not at all" is expressed by حیچ (hīch). Ex.:

Ottoman TurkishTransliterationTranslation
حیچ چیزی یوق دور hīch chīzī yoq dour He has not any money at all
حیچ حیز دیگل چاش هاستا در hīch chīz degil çāş hāstā dir He has not any money at all

"How many?"

"How many?" is expressed by قاچ (qaçh?). Ex.:

EnglishOttoman TurkishTransliteration
How many pastries have you? قاچ بورکك وار در qaçh börekın var dır?
How many books has he? قاچ کتابك وار در qaçh kitabın var dır?

"How much?"

"How much?" is expressed by نقدر (né qadar?); as:

EnglishOttoman TurkishTransliteration
How much sugar have you? نقدر شکرك وار در né qadar şekerın var dır?
How much bread have we? نقدر آکمکمز وار در né qadar ékmegımız var dır?

"Some"

"Some" is expressed by برآز (bir az) "a little", برقدر (bir qadar) "a little, a tiny; of anything" in referring to inanimate objects; as:

EnglishOttoman TurkishTransliteration
some bread برآز آکمک bir az ékmek

But in reference to animate objects باضی (ba'zı) گاچ (qarh) is used; as:

EnglishOttoman TurkishTransliteration
some people باضی آدملر ba'zı ādemler
some gentlemen برگاچ افندیلر bir gaçh efendıler
some animals باضی حیوانلر ba'zı hayvanlar

"Both"

"Both" is rendered by هم — هم (hem — hem); as:

EnglishOttoman TurkishTransliteration
I have both bread and salt بنم هم آکمک هم ملح وار در benim hem ékmek hem milh var dir
My aunt has both paper and pen خالهم هم کاغد هم قلم وار در khalem hem kāghad hem qalem var dir

"Either ... or ..." and "Neither ... nor ..."

"Either ... or ..." is rendered by یا...یا (ya...ya...);

"Neither ... nor ..." is rendered by نه...نه (né...né); as:

EnglishOttoman TurkishTransliteration
I have neither bread nor salt بنده نه آکمک نه نمک وار در bende né ékmek né nemek var dir
You have either pen or paper سنده یا قلم یا کاغد وار در sende ya qalem ya kāghad var dir
