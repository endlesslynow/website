1. Introduction to Izafét

This lesson covers how possession or connection between things is expressed in Ottoman Turkish through a grammatical concept called "Izafét" اضافت, which means "addition or annexation."

The Izafét is a grammatical structure in Ottoman Turkish used to show relationships between nouns - especially possession, composition, or connection. Think of it as different ways to express phrases like "the door of the house," "a golden box," or "apple tree" in English.

2. The Three Types of Izafét

1. Material Relationship

This is when you want to say something is made of a material.

In English: "gold box," "silk handkerchief," "silver watch"

How it works: Just put the material word before the object, with no changes to either word.

Ottoman Transliteration English
آلتون قوطو altoun qoutou a golden box
ايپك منديل ipék méndil a silk handkerchief
گومش ساعت gü-müsh sa'at a silver watch

Alternatively, you can put the material noun in the ablative case:

Ottoman Transliteration English
آلتوندن كوستك altoundan kösték a chain of gold
الماسدن بيلزيك élmasdan biléz'ik a bracelet of diamond

2. Indefinite Relationship

This is used for general categories or types, like "house door" (any door of any house).

In English: "house door," "well water," "pear tree"

How it works: Put the two nouns together, but add a possessive suffix (-i or -si) to the second noun.

Ottoman Transliteration English
او قپوسی év qapousou a house-door (indefinite)
قويو صويی qouyou souyou well water
آرمود آغاجی armoud a-ghaji pear tree

This form is also used for names of countries, rivers, mountains, cities, etc.

Ottoman Transliteration English
سيواس شهری Sivas shéhri The city of Sivas
طونه نهری Touna néhri The river Danube

3. Definite Possession

This shows specific ownership, like "John's car" or "the door of THE house."

In English: "the door of the house," "the tree of the apple," "the water of the well"

How it works: Add a genitive ending to the first noun AND a possessive suffix to the second noun.

Ottoman Transliteration English
اوك قپوسی évin qapousou The door of the house (definite)
الماسك آغاجی élmanin a-ghaji The tree of the apple
قويونك صويی qou-younoun souyou The water of the well

This third type is the most "complete" form of possession and is most similar to English possessive structures like "John's book" or "the cat's tail."

Summary of the Three Types

1. Material Relationship: Simply put words together (gold box)

2. Indefinite Relationship: First word unchanged + second word with possessive suffix (like saying "apple its-tree")

3. Definite Possession: First word in genitive + second word with possessive suffix (like saying "house's its-door")

3. Special Constructions

Quantity Expressions

When the two nouns come together in English, with the word of between them, the first expressing the quantity of the second, it is translated into Ottoman by simply putting the name of the quantity before the other noun and omitting 'of' as German they say 'Eine Flasche Wein', 'a bottle of wine'.

Ottoman Transliteration Translation
بر فنجان چای bir finjan chay a cup of tea
اوچ اوقيه شكر üch oqiyé shékér three okes of sugar
اون آرشين بز on arshin béz ten yards of cloth
يوز اولچك بوغداى yüz ölchék boughday a hundred bushels of wheat
بر سورو قويون bir sürü qoyoun a flock of sheep
Historical Note: An oke (also spelled "oka" or "ocque") is an old unit of weight used throughout the Ottoman Empire and parts of the Middle East and North Africa. One oke of sugar equals approximately 1.28 kilograms or about 2.8 pounds. This traditional measurement unit varied slightly by region but was commonly used for weighing agricultural products and commodities like sugar before the widespread adoption of the metric system. Though largely obsolete today, it may still appear in historical recipes or in some traditional markets in former Ottoman territories.

Construction with Cardinal Numbers

The following construction is frequent between a noun and a cardinal number.

Ottoman Transliteration Translation
كتابك دوردی kitabin dördi four of the books
اوطه‌نك ايكيسی o-da-nin ikisi two of the rooms

Declension of Izafét Constructions

When using an Izafét construction in a sentence, you need to add case endings to express different grammatical relationships. In Ottoman Turkish, case endings are added to the end of the second word (the possessed item), while the first word (the possessor) remains unchanged in its genitive form.

The key point is that when using an Izafét construction in a sentence, you keep the first word in its possessive form and only add case endings to the second word (the possessed item). This allows you to use the whole phrase in different grammatical roles.

Case Ottoman Transliteration English Meaning
Basic Form اوك قپوسی évin qapousou the door of the house
Genitive اوك قپوسی‌نك évin qapousou-noun of the door of the house
Dative اوك قپوسی‌نه évin qapousou-na to the door of the house
Accusative اوك قپوسی‌نی évin qapousou-nou the door of the house (as object)
Locative اوك قپوسی‌نده évin qapousou-nda in/at the door of the house
Ablative اوك قپوسی‌ندن évin qapousou-ndan from the door of the house

-dé and Da-khí

The suffix -dé

There are two wordsده-dé in Ottoman; one is used with the nouns in the Locative case, and is always accented:اودهév'dé,باغدهbaghdá 'in the house, in the vineyard'.

The otherده-dé is a conjunction, meaning 'also, and': it is never connected with the noun, nor is it accented; that is, the accent is at the end of the preceding word; as:او دهév' dé',باغ دهbagh' da',پدر دهpédér' dé',بنم دهbénim' dé', meaning 'The house also, the vineyard too, my father also, mine also'.

The suffix Da-khí

دخیDa-khí is also used with the same meaning ('also, too'); as:بنده دخیbéndé dakhí,سنده دخیséndé dakhí 'in me also, in thee too'.
