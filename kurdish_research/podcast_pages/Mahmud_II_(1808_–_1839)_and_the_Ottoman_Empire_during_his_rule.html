Mahmud II (1808 – 1839) and the Ottoman Empire during his rule

Download Audio File

Description

The source provides a detailed historical overview of the 31-year reign of Sultan Mahmud II (1808-1839), positioning him as the autocratic architect of the modern Ottoman Empire. It explains that his traumatic ascension and political education led him to ruthlessly prioritize centralization and the elimination of rival power centers. His greatest internal success was the Vaka-i Hayriye (Auspicious Incident) of 1826, which involved the destruction of the Janissary corps, clearing the path for administrative, military, and social reforms like the establishment of new ministries and the adoption of the fez. However, the text also emphasizes a "vulnerability paradox," as this necessary internal restructuring led to profound external defeats against Russia, the Great Powers, and, most humiliatingly, his own rebellious vassal, Muhammad Ali of Egypt, though he ultimately laid the foundational structures for the subsequent Tanzimat era.
