A short note from your cultural attaché.

You hold the only authorized field guide for civilian travel to ancient and modern Baghdad. Within these pages you will find sights worth photographing, smells worth bottling, sounds worth recording, and warnings worth heeding.

Baghdad has lived 1,300 years on the bend of the Tigris, and every century has its own perfume. Some you will want on your wrist for the rest of your life. Some you must rinse off in the river before you return home. This guide tells you which is which. Read it twice before stepping into the chamber. The Tigris bends, and so does fortune.

We arrange itineraries for travelers of every persuasion. Some want only the smell of fresh paper at the House of Wisdom. Others insist on being present for a banquet at the Palace of the Tree. Fees vary. Insurance does not.

ERA 01 OF 10 PREHISTORIC BAGHDADU 2000 BCE

Mud, river silt, woodsmoke
at dawn

A handful of mud-brick dwellings tucked among reed beds. There is no city here yet, only the rumour of one. Bring sturdy boots, a notebook, and very low expectations of cuisine.

📅 c. 2000 BCE 📍 Banks of the Tigris VIBE Quiet farmstead
SAFE
PLATE 1.1 Reed houses beside the Tigris
👁 SIGHTS

Low mud-brick dwellings the colour of the earth they stand on. Cattle drinking at the river's edge. Children running fishing nets between reed clumps. The Tigris itself, brown with silt, glittering at dusk.

👃 SMELLS

Wet river silt, damp grass, and the soft woodsmoke of cooking fires. Drying fish in the afternoon. Cattle, unmissably so. The clean, mineral smell of rain on warm earth, which the locals call the breath of the river.

👂 SOUNDS

The rush of the Tigris. The soft slap of nets on water. A goat bell. The hollow tap of a wooden mortar.

★ HIGHLY RECOMMENDED

Stand on the riverbank at first light. The air will smell of nothing but water and mud. You will not have this experience again in this guide.

✕ AVOID

Drinking unfiltered river water. Trying to ask anyone for the way to "Baghdad". The settlement is named Baghdadu and is roughly the size of a livestock pen.

ERA 02 OF 10 FOUNDATION DAY 30 JULY 762 CE

CINDERS
trace a circle
on the desert floor

Caliph Al-Mansur scouts the river himself, picks the bend, and orders the lines of his perfect circular capital traced into the soil with cotton balls soaked in naphtha and set alight. The ground itself glows. Then the bricklayers arrive, and they do not stop for forty years.

📅 762 → 800 CE 📍 Madinat al-Salaam VIBE Construction site of the millennium
HOT TIP
PLATE 2.1 Plan of Madinat al-Salaam, after Yakubi

FIELD TIP · THE FIRST BRICK

The royal astrologers chose 30 July as the most auspicious date in living memory. Arrive a day early to watch the foundation lines glow at dusk, traced in burning cotton soaked in naphtha. It is the most cinematic moment in urban history.

The architect-caliph stands at the centre of a circle 2 kilometres across, his palace at the bullseye, four iron gates at the cardinal points, and a triple wall forty feet thick between him and the world. The bricks weigh up to 200 pounds each. Workers haul them on their backs. Chroniclers say there are 100,000 of them. Believe half.

👁 SIGHTS

A swarming hive of architects, kiln workers, and surveyors. Mountains of sun-baked clay. The four iron gates, said to have been forged by demons for King Solomon. A green dome 48 metres tall above the Caliph's palace, visible from every approach to the city.

👃 SMELLS

Freshly fired clay. Excavated earth. The metallic tang of the coppersmiths' alley in Karkh. The dry, grassy scent of the reed-weavers. Pungent camel stables at the city edge, which you will smell before you see them.

👂 SOUNDS

Hammers on stone, all day. Hooves of mule trains arriving from Khurasan. The shouted orders of foremen in Arabic, Persian, Aramaic, and Greek, sometimes all at once.

★ DO THIS

Cross the river by guffa. The circular reed coracles bob like teacups and a single ride costs two kirats. Ask for a sailboat carved as a lion if you are feeling theatrical.

✕ AVOID

Wandering near a brick haul. A 200-pound brick falls only once. Also, do not flatter Al-Mansur. He is suspicious by nature and your modern English will mark you as a spy.

ERA 03 OF 10 THE GOLDEN AGE 786 → 809 CE

Roses, paper, mechanical birds,
and a great deal of saffron

If you can only afford one stop on your itinerary, make it Harun al-Rashid's Baghdad. This is the city as the world remembers it. The lodestar of scientists, poets, and merchants. Pack your softest linen and an empty stomach.

📅 786 → 809 CE 📍 Royal Precinct, House of Wisdom, Karkh Bazaars VIBE A Thousand and One Nights, with documentation

👁 SIGHTS

Gold plates encrusted with precious stones. Guests in jewel-studded slippers. The Palace of the Tree, a 1.5-ton mechanical contraption of gold and silver whose eighteen branches bear birds that whistle and sing on command, leaves rustling as if a breeze had passed. Byzantine envoys arrive impressed and leave terrified.

👃 SMELLS

Musk, saffron, and rosewater, blended in silver urns and used to scent the seven tunics of fine Rashidi linen the Caliph wears each day. Spiced stews of onions and marrows. Roasting sheep. Kunafa, golden, dripping honey, sticky on every smile in the room. Most of all, in a corner of the city you will smell something faint and revolutionary, the scent of fresh ink and new paper sheaves at the House of Wisdom.

👂 SOUNDS

Crystal goblets ringing with ruby Shiraz wine. The thrum of a lute behind a screen. The murmur of Christian, Jewish, Muslim, and Zoroastrian scholars translating Aristotle in the same room.

"Cairo writes,
Beirut prints,
Baghdad reads."
2.4 House of Wisdom, paper from China, 794

★ EAT THIS

A meal for two. Roast meat, wheat bread dripping with sauce, an almond pie, iced water from the mountains, and a cup of Shiraz. Total 20 daniqs, well within budget for any Tempora client travelling on the silver-tier package.

FIELD TIP · THE PERFUMER'S STALL

Bring a small empty vial. The perfumer near the Karkh gate sells ten different scented waters. Ask for orange-flower, then for sweet-scented willow. Both will outlive you, and both can be smuggled home in your sleeve if the customs officer is in a good mood.

"Sticky smiles, ruby goblets, and the new smell of paper everywhere."
A field reporter, returned 798 CE

ERA 04 OF 10 THE CRIMSON TIDE 811 → 1401 CE

Naphtha, ink, and
ninety thousand skulls

Three appalling moments deserve your awareness, and your absolute avoidance. We list them so that you may calibrate your time-machine to skip past them and not, under any circumstances, accidentally arrive in their middle.

📅 811 → 1401 CE 📍 All quarters, all bridges, all four gates VIBE HARD SKIP
⚠ DANGER
PLATE 4.1 Kala Chiya, Maidan Square, after Tamerlane 1401

✕ SIEGE OF 811-813

The fratricidal war between Amin and Mamun. Mangonels hurl stones like men's heads, the Harbiya quarter is choked with naphtha smoke, and the Tigris becomes a repository for the decapitated. The smell of burning palm groves carries for miles.

SEVERITY 09 / 10

✕ MONGOL SACK · JANUARY 1258

Hulagu Khan ends the Abbasid Caliphate in a fortnight. Up to two million dead. The libraries of the House of Wisdom are emptied into the river. Witnesses describe the Tigris running black with ink for days, then red.

SEVERITY 10 / 10

✕ TAMERLANE · 1401

Each soldier is ordered to bring his commander a head. The result, called Kala Chiya by locals, is 120 towers built from 90,000 severed skulls, ringed around Maidan Square. There is nothing here for you. Move along.

SEVERITY 10 / 10
👃 THE SMELL OF THESE CENTURIES

Acrid woodsmoke. The metallic tang of blood. Stagnant floodwater when the Isa Canal breached its banks. The peculiar leathery scent of bodies partially embalmed in resin and hung on the three pontoon bridges as a deterrent of state justice. We mention this only because the historian must, and because no time traveler should ever experience it.

If your itinerary lands you here, return to the chamber. Tempora Travel will issue a credit, no questions asked.

ERA 05 OF 10 SHOUTING IS SPEAKING 1601 CE

The arrival of coffee,
hookahs, and the longest
argument of the century

The first Baghdadi coffeehouses open in the early 17th century and have not closed since. The local saying explains everything you need to know about the room you are about to enter. Shouting is speaking.

📅 1601 onward 📍 Coffeehouses across the Ottoman quarters VIBE Smoky, intense, friendly
backgammon clatter,
hookah bubbles,
politics
5.1 Interior of an Ottoman-era coffeehouse, c. 1700
👁 SIGHTS

Dark, wood-panelled rooms. Men playing beads, bent over backgammon boards. Hookahs taller than children. Walls hung with portraits of poets you have never heard of and saints you have only read about.

👃 SMELLS

The sharp, bitter aroma of beans roasting in iron pans, cutting through thick clouds of sweet apple-flavoured tobacco smoke. A hint of cardamom in every cup, and a hint of yesterday's argument in every chair.

👂 SOUNDS

The bubble of water pipes. The clack of backgammon pieces. The voices of three men talking at the same volume about the same topic and somehow understanding each other.

★ DO THIS

Order a small cup of qahwa, a glass of cold water, and stay for at least an hour. Do not pay attention to the political topic. The argument is recreational. Whoever pays for the next round wins it.

FIELD TIP · ETIQUETTE

Stand when an elder enters. Decline the third cup of coffee with your hand on your heart. The fourth cup is a trap.

VI
ERA 06 OF 10 THE BLACK ODOUR SPRING 1831

An empty city and a smell
that will follow you home

We mention this year only so that you may avoid it. Even an inoculated traveler cannot fully escape the somatic memory of an entire city in mourning.

📅 February → August 1831 📍 Every quarter, but mostly the river districts VIBE HARD SKIP

The 1831 outbreak emptied the streets within a fortnight. Physicians of the era described the affliction by what they called its "black odour", a foul scent of corrupt blood that the public mind came to associate with the very transmission of death. There is no recommended itinerary for this year.

✕ HARD SKIP

Avoid every date between February and August of 1831. Avoid any year ending in epidemic regardless of period. This includes outbreaks of cholera, smallpox, and the recurring fevers of the lower Tigris. Tempora Travel disclaims all responsibility for travelers who insist.

ERA 07 OF 10 THE DRAGGING OF THE ROPE 1916 → 1958

Pastrami, perfume, neon,
and the staccato
click of scissors

In 1916 the Ottoman military stretches two ropes across the city and demolishes everything in between. The result is Rashid Street. The poor lose their homes, the wealthy keep theirs, and the smell of pulverised masonry hangs in the air for months. From this rubble grows the most glamorous boulevard in the Arab world.

📅 1916 → 1958 CE 📍 Al-Rashid Street, Taht el Takia, Shorja VIBE Cosmopolitan, mid-century, contradictory
FIRST TRAFFIC LIGHT, 1956
7.1 The first traffic light in Iraq is installed here, 1956
👁 SIGHTS

Orosdi-Back's Harrods-style window displays. The first neon signs in the country, glowing red after dusk. Boutiques selling French perfumes and Egyptian fabrics. The arched colonnades of the boulevard, repeating into a long perspective. Gramophones in cafe windows, playing the singers Salima Murad and Munira al-Mahdiyya.

👃 SMELLS

Freshly ground coffee. The pungent, spicy scent of basterma, smoked beef hung in delicatessen windows. Arshak's expensive French perfume wafting from the door of his portrait studio. The leaded petrol of imported London double-decker buses, against your better judgement, smelling somehow exciting.

👂 SOUNDS

The staccato clicking of barbers' scissors. The long raspberries blown by coachmen like the legendary Sheykhan. The clack of backgammon pieces in Al Zahawi cafe. A live broadcast of the Iraqi maqam performed by Yusuf Za'rur, drifting from a radio shop.

★ DO THIS (1948)

Take a window table at Cafe Brazilian on a Thursday evening. Order coffee, listen to the radio, and watch the boulevard fill with families heading to the cinemas. Tip generously. The waiter will remember.

✕ AVOID

Lingering in central Baghdad on 14 July 1958. The "ugly" transition of the coup unfolds quickly, and the very streets that hosted royal parades become something far less hospitable within hours.

SEVERITY 08 / 10

HISTORICAL FOOTNOTE

Iraq's first traffic light is installed at the corner of Rashid Street in 1956. Locals stop to watch it change for the first three weeks. So should you.

ERA 08 OF 10 OIL, FISH, AND CONTRADICTION 1968 → 1980

Masgouf grilling on the river,
while the sarifa
neighborhoods drown

Oil money rebuilds the centre in marble and concrete. Ten kilometres away, tens of thousands live in sarifa reed-and-mud houses on the city's edge. Both districts share the same sky. Both share the same Tigris. The smells are very different.

📅 1968 → 1980 CE 📍 Abu Nuwas riverside, outer ring of Sadr City VIBE Glittering centre, painful periphery
EAT THIS
8.1 Masgouf on the Abu Nuwas riverbank, summer evening
👁 SIGHTS

Wide avenues filled with imported luxury vehicles. A flourishing intellectual cafe culture along Abu Nuwas Street. Multi-generational families feasting at long damask-covered tables. Public gardens in flower. On the city's outskirts, an ocean of mud-and-reed sarifa houses, in stark visual contrast to the air-conditioned ministries of the centre.

👃 SMELLS

Grilling masgouf, the river fish split open and slow-cooked over orangewood, served with flatbreads and a mango sauce that will redefine your understanding of mango. Public garden jasmine in bloom. The leaded gasoline of state-imported sedans. In the sarifa neighborhoods, raw sewage, damp earth, and animal husbandry, all of it unfiltered by infrastructure.

★ EAT THIS

Masgouf at sunset on Abu Nuwas Street. The fish is split open like a book, salted, then propped vertically beside an open fire of orangewood. It cooks for an hour. You wait. You will not regret waiting.

✕ AVOID

Walking into the sarifa neighborhoods at night without a guide. Conditions are difficult and politely guarded. Any unsolicited photography will be received as an insult.

Air-raid sirens, blast walls,
and the smell of charred books

A single generation lives through three wars and a decade of sanctions. We list the sensory landmarks of the era so that future travelers may understand the scale of what was endured, and what survived anyway.

📅 1980 → 2007 CE 📍 Citywide. Mutanabbi Street, especially. VIBE WITNESS ONLY

WITNESS ONLY
9.1 From a memoir of Baghdad, 1985, on the sound of the red alert siren
👁 1980s · IRAN-IRAQ WAR

Sights of anti-aircraft tracer fire arcing across the night sky. Citizens huddled in dark interior hallways reading novels by candlelight. The somatic, body-shaking concussion of distant strikes felt as much as heard.

👃 APRIL 2003 · THE INVASION

The smell of cordite, pulverised concrete, and burning documents from looted ministries. Helicopters, in the words of one Baghdadi, hanging low like angry wasps. Rooms with no air conditioning because the grid is gone.

👂 2006-2007 · SECTARIAN WALLS

Three-mile-long grey concrete blast walls rise overnight, physically amputating neighborhoods from one another. Old districts like Taht el Takia become deserted warrens governed by, in one journalist's phrase, a febrile atmosphere of justified paranoia.

📖 5 MARCH 2007 · MUTANABBI STREET

A suicide bomb detonates in the heart of the book market. Charred pages drift down the alley like grey leaves. Within months, booksellers are back. Within a year, Shahbandar Coffee-House reopens. The street insists on existing.

"Cairo writes, Beirut prints, and Baghdad reads."
Arab proverb, last verified Friday morning at the Mutanabbi book market

ERA 10 OF 10 THE INTELLECTUAL LUNGS 2026 CE

The smell of fresh books,
sweet sheesha, and
grilled fish, all at once

Baghdad in our own century is the city refusing to disappear. You will hear coppersmith hammers ringing in alleys older than your country. You will smell paper and tea on the same breeze. You will see old men by the river at dusk, drinking tea, exactly as their ancestors did.

👁 SIGHTS

Hundreds of booksellers lining Al-Mutanabbi Street, with photocopied editions stacked beside hard-to-find originals. The Al-Shaheed Monument's turquoise dome split down the centre, an eternal flame at its core. Old men sipping black tea beside the river at dusk. The interior of Shahbandar Cafe, walls covered in memorabilia of a hundred years of Baghdadi political life.

👃 SMELLS

Sweet sheesha smoke. Piping hot black tea served in istikans, small tulip-shaped glasses. The perennial aroma of grilled masgouf drifting up from Abu Nuwas. Aging paper, ink, and the faint vinegar of book glue. Pomegranate and tamarind juice from the Haji Zbala stand.

👂 SOUNDS

The rhythmic, melodic ding of coppersmith hammers, a sound continuous for over a thousand years. Poetic recitations from a microphone strapped to a chair. The small tink of a teaspoon against an istikan. The laughter of children playing by the river.

★ FRIDAY ITINERARY

Begin at sunrise on Mutanabbi Street. Browse for an hour. Buy something you cannot read yet. Walk three blocks to Souq al-Safafeer for a copper tray. Stop at Shahbandar for tea and one cigarette of someone else's tobacco. Cross to Abu Nuwas for masgouf at sunset. Sleep on the train home.

FIELD TIP · THE FOURTH GLASS OF TEA

Refuse it politely once. Accept it on the second offer. The hospitality of Baghdad is a centuries-deep tradition and your refusal will be taken as a request to argue.

"The city refuses to die. Bring an empty bag."
A Tempora bookseller, March 2026

💰 CURRENCY & PRICES

VERIFIED 9TH-CENTURY DATA
1 silver dirham
3 grams of silver. Standard daily wage for a porter. Buys a flatbread, a fish, and a smile.
1 gold dinar
Roughly 20 dirhams. Saved for taxes, dowries, and bribes of an unspecified ceremonial nature.
2 kirats
A single ride across the city by donkey from the Karkh stand. Tip the donkey-keeper, not the donkey.
20 daniqs
A meal for two. Roast meat, sauced bread, almond pie, iced water. Highest possible value-per-daniq in the empire.
1 small glass of tea
Free, in any century, in any cafe, after the third polite refusal of the fourth glass.

💬 USEFUL PHRASES

POCKET REFERENCE
As-salaamu alaykum
Peace be upon you. Universal greeting. Works in 762 and 2026.
Shukran
Thank you. Use after every cup of tea. Use even before the cup of tea.
La, shukran
No, thank you. Required for the fourth cup of tea. Will be ignored.
Wayn al-souq?
Where is the market? The answer is always "two streets that way", whichever way you are facing.
Yallah!
Let's go. Said with affection by anyone who has waited longer than ninety seconds for anything.
Habibi
My dear. Used by waiters, taxi drivers, and old friends. Use sparingly. Or do not.
