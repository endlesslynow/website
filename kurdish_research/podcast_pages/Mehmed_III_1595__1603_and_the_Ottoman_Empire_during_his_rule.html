Mehmed III 1595 1603 and the Ottoman Empire during his rule

Description

The provided source is an extensive academic report detailing the tumultuous reign of Sultan Mehmed III (1595–1603), presenting it as a pivotal era defined by acute crisis and systemic transformation within the Ottoman Empire. The text first examines the Sultan's paradoxical character, noting his piety and military victory at Keresztes, alongside his brutality in executing nineteen brothers and his own son to ensure dynastic security, thus becoming the last sultan trained under the traditional provincial system. Institutionally, the source analyzes how the pressures of the costly Long Turkish War in Europe and the devastating Jelali Rebellions in Anatolia—fueled by climate change, inflation, and the decline of the feudal *sipahi* cavalry—forced the collapse of the classical Ottoman state model. Ultimately, the report highlights the shift in governance toward the powerful Valide Sultan Safiye and the establishment of the Kafes confinement system for princes, signaling the definitive end of the old order and the forging of new, often fragile, 17th-century institutions.
