Description

The provided source is an extensive historical report analyzing the two brief and chaotic reigns of Sultan Mustafa I (1617–1618 and 1622–1623), framing him as a symptom of deep institutional crises within the Ottoman Empire during the early 17th century. The text emphasizes that Mustafa I, who was mentally incapacitated by years of confinement in the Kafes (princely cage), was merely a puppet figurehead whose enthronements and depositions were dictated by ferocious power struggles among competing court factions. Critically, the source argues that the period marked a violent transformation of the state, establishing the principle of agnatic seniority (succession by the oldest male) and permanently diffusing power to the Imperial Harem (notably Halime and Kösem Sultans) and the Janissary corps. This political paralysis led directly to the regicide of Osman II and severe external setbacks, including the loss of Baghdad to the Safavids and intensified provincial rebellions, demonstrating a total collapse of central authority.
